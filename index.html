All you need to know about BiDi languages

Which countries and how many people speak Arabic and Hebrew

More than 600 million people in fast-developing regions are using scripts that are written mostly in right-to-left (RTL for short) direction. The Arabic language is native for over 295 million people. There are over 5 million Hebrew native speakers throughout the world. Also, there are over 310 million people who speak other languages with scripts based on Arabic alphabet. Esri supports localization of Arabic (AR) and Hebrew (HE), but our products should support (accept input) in all RTL languages. In this article we will focus mostly on AR and HE, but most of the rules and information here apply to other BiDi languages as well.

Several years ago, Arabic was not widely used on internet. In 2015, it did not feature even in the top ten languages used on the internet. It has grown to be the fourth most used language on the internet by now. There are more than 168.1 million Arabic-speaking internet users worldwide. The language just misses out on the top three spots, but it can change in the near future.

Why these languages are called bidirectional

Text written in Hebrew and Arabic languages is often referred to as bidirectional, or BiDi for short. Arabic and Hebrew are written predominantly right to left (RTL for short). Numbers are written with the most significant digit on the left, just as in English or other left-to-right text.

Languages written in left-to-right scripts—for example, product names or citations—are often inserted, so the complete document is bidirectional in nature, a mix of both right-to-left and left-to-right writing.

BiDi languages are rarely used vertically. The only intuitive way to achieve vertical BiDi script is to start with a standard line of horizontal RTL text and rotate it 90 degrees counterclockwise so that it points downward.

Reading right to left means the whole experience—not just the line of text—is right to left. Books are alphabetized on a shelf RTL. Books open from what Westerners would consider the back cover. A sequence of instructional steps must go in RTL direction.

Application and web page layouts require mirroring or flipping. That means that what was aligned right becomes aligned left and vice versa. Instead of aligning left it is better to use term “beginning” and instead of right – “end”.

What affects text direction

The order of characters in memory (logical) is not the same as the order in which they are displayed (visual). The set of rules applied by the software to produce the correct order at the time of display are described by the Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm, or BiDi algorithm for short.

How does the software know whether this is a sequence of left-to-right or right-to-left characters? Because each character in Unicode has an associated directional property. Most letters are strongly typed as LTR (left to right). Letters from right-to-left scripts are strongly typed as RTL (right to left).

The order in which text is displayed depends on the base direction assigned to the phrase, paragraph, or block that contains it.

Spaces and punctuation are not strongly typed as either LTR or RTL, because they may be used in either type of script. They are therefore classed as neutral or weak characters. A neutral character between two strongly typed characters that have the same directional type will also assume that directionality.

More information about strong and weak characters can be found in Unicode Bidirectional Algorithm basics


Letter Forms and Ligatures

BiDi scripts are inherently cursive, which means that each BiDi script has multiple letter forms, depending whether it’s used at the start, middle, or end of the word—and there’s a stand-alone (detached) form. Both Hebrew and Arabic alphabets have 24 characters. Note that some characters should be written differently depending on their position in the word (beginning, middle, end, and stand-alone). Hebrew has only five letters with both a normal form and a final form. In Arabic script, certain letters can only connect with other letters on the right side but not the left. This creates slight breaks in the script flow that are subtly smaller than breaks between words. Sometimes browsers like Internet Explorer 11 and Edge display Arabic text as disjoined characters; it is a bug.

Arabic letters can also take different shapes, or ligatures, depending on the other letters around them. The letters are combined, or stacked, in different ways, based on which letters are being written.

Punctuation Symbols

Punctuation Symbols appear at the left side of a text, and sometimes are different from English.

For instance, a question mark in Hebrew looks the same as in English but goes on the left side: “?שְׁאֵלָה”. In Arabic, it goes on the left side and is turned in the opposite direction: “سؤال؟”

The percent sign goes on the left side of the number in Arabic (%100), but not in Hebrew (100%).

Numerals and math

The Arabic-Indic numerals are decimal-based numbers used in Arabic- and Farsi-speaking countries. They are one of the oldest sets of numerals still in use today, though the Western Arabic system used in European languages has become dominant in recent centuries. Although Arabic text is written right to left, numbers are written the same way as in left-to-right languages, with the most significant digit on the left. The number 123 (one hundred and twenty-three) is written ١٢٣ (123, not 321).

In Hebrew today, the decimal system of Western Arabic numerals (for example, 0, 1, 2, 3) is used in almost all cases, with an exception of the calendar, which will be discussed later in this document.

Arabic-Indic is used in some, but not all, Arabic-speaking countries. ArcGIS Pro supports both Western Arabic and Arabic-Indic numerals. Native mobile applications are reusing numerals from the system settings. Currently web apps are supposed to display only Western Arabic numerals. Some browsers display a mixture of Western Arabic and Arabic-Indic numbers, but it is better to avoid such behavior.

Date formats and calendars

Numbers are written LTR; text flow is RTL. That means that if, for instance, a date is in dd/mm/yyyy format, the result will be 2019/12/31. Use third-party libraries or Intl objects that support CLDR, and use RTL tags.

Hebrew calendar

The Hebrew calendar, also called the Jewish calendar, is a lunisolar calendar used today predominantly for Jewish religious observances. It determines the dates for Jewish holidays and the appropriate public reading of Torah portions and daily psalm readings, among many ceremonial uses. In Israel, it is used for religious purposes, provides a time frame for agriculture, and is an official calendar for civil purposes, although the latter usage has been steadily declining in favor of the Gregorian calendar.

Arabic calendar

The Hijri (Islamic) calendar is based on the motion of the moon. It started after the Hijrah of the Prophet (PBUH) to Medina. The beginning of each month is contingent on the visibility of the moon at the end of the previous month. Once the moon is sighted, the new month commences. Each month starts with a new lunar cycle. Hence, the Hijri calendar gives only a tentative overview of the upcoming Islamic dates as the start of each month is subject to the sighting of the moon. The Hijri calendar consists of 12 months similar to the Gregorian calendar. However, it consists of 354–355 days, unlike the 365–366 days in the Gregorian calendar.

The Umm al-Quara calendar is a variation on Hijri. The first day of every month is based on astronomical computation. This is usually the same as the Hijri calendar but can be one or two days earlier. It is used as a civil calendar in Saudi Arabia; it is the Windows default.

What to mirror

Directional-sensitive graphics

Directional-sensitive graphics (Undo/Redo, Next/Previous, and so on) are mirrored for RTL layout, so their meaning becomes the opposite.

Arrows meaning when Mirroring
Left Right
LTR: Backward, Previous, Undo LTR: Forward, Next, Redo
RTL: Forward, Next RTL: Backward, Previous

Coordinates

The latitude-longitude coordinates are written as longitude-latitude (opposite to English), but because of the general RTL text direction, longitude appears at the beginning of the string on the right, and latitude appears at the end of the string on the left.

English latitude/longitude direction 119°38'53.53''W, 36°19'22.94''N
Arabic latitude/longitude direction W°119'38''53.53 ,N °36'19''22.94
Hebrew latitude/longitude direction 119°38'53.53''W, 36°19'22.94''N

Note: Not all countries use N/S/E/W notation similar to English. Countries where Arabic is used refer to the cardinal directions as Ash Shamaliyah (N), Al Gharbiyah (W), Ash Sharqiyah (E) and Al Janobiyah (S). Additionally, Al Wusta is used for the center. For instance, N is written in AR as ثوانی

Do not hardcode N/S/E/W.



Lists and Legends

Legends, ordered lists and unordered lists should be right-aligned, with bullets or patches on the right. The same is true for icons. For a numbered list icon, don't mirror it, a special icon exists in Calcite-components. (In Desktop apps like ArcGIS Pro cartographer is able to align legend patches other way if needed for cartographic purposes or visual hierarchy).

Whenever possible, provide an option for the user to change the legend RTL/LTR flow manually if needed.

Menus, popups, and drop-down lists

Dialog box layouts

The order of most UI elements should go in RTL direction in Arabic and Hebrew UI. Dialog boxes should be mirrored.

While tools within their panels are following their LTR or RTL order, there is an exception. Widgets within the map frame are always positioned the same, no matter RTL or LTR.

What not to mirror

Share, Import, Export, and Download icons

The Share, Import, Export, Download icons should look just like LTR; they do not have directional meaning. If you are not sure whether icons should be mirrored, check with other applications such as Microsoft Outlook, Word, AutoCAD, and Adobe Illustrator to find out how to render graphics and icons in RTL.

Nondirectional graphics, images, trademarks, Esri styles and logos

Graphics should not be mirrored at all, such as legal trademarks or logos, because they do not make sense when laid out from right to left.

Image elements, especially all raster imageries, should not be mirrored; otherwise, they will become unusable and distorted.

Text reading order in the graphics should honor LTR or RTL. It should not blindly mirror everything.

Charts, graphs, and histograms

Charts and histograms should be the same as in English, except the Hebrew and Arabic text reading order should be RTL and the alignment should be to the right. The legend is neutral on the graph and chart. It does not have directional meaning, so the symbol can be on either side, but the numeric value that contains greater than symbols (>) or less than symbols (<) (for example, >1000, < 999) should honor LTR reading order.

URLs, file paths, and English text

Keep URLs, file paths, and English text LTR. In some cases, they can be right-aligned, but it is not required.

Sometimes English text with punctuation may look confusing in RTL layouts. While there is no way to control user-defined text (which may be typed in any language), it is better to control the punctuation in text, which is always in English, using LTR tags. See the following example:

Contextual Digits

Contextual Digits – ٠١٢٣٤ABCDE67890

If you have a string like 12345ABCDE67890, and you render it on an Arabic system, you might get ٠١٢٣٤ABCDE67890. The leading digits are rendered as Arabic-Indic digits, but the trailing digits are rendered as Western Arabic digits.

This is a feature known as contextual digit substitution. You can specify whether Western Arabic digits are replaced with native equivalents by going to the Region control panel (formerly known as Regional and Language Options), clicking on the Formats tab, going to Additional settings (formerly known as Customize this format), and looking at the options under Use native digits. The three options there correspond to the three values for LOCAL_IDIGITSUBSTITUTION. Programmatically, you can override the user preference (if you know that you are in a special case, like an IP address) by following the instructions in MSDN.

  • Uniscribe: Script­Apply­Digit­Substitution
  • DWrite: IDWrite­Text­Analysis­Sink::Set­Number­Substitution
  • GDI: ETO_NUMERICS­LATIN or ETO_NUMERICS­LOCAL

As a last resort, you can stick a Unicode NODS (U+206F) at the beginning of the string to force Western Arabic digits, or a Unicode NADS (U+206E) to force national digits. Bonus chatter: What’s the point of contextual digit substitution anyway? Suppose you have the string “there are 3 items remaining.” (Let’s say that all text in lowercase is in Arabic.) You want this 3 to be rendered in Arabic-Indic digits because it is part of an Arabic sentence. On the other hand, if you have the string “that’s a really nice BMW 350.” you want the 350 to be in Western Arabic digits since it is part of the brand name “BMW 350”.

Contextual digit substitution chooses whether to use Arabic-Indic digits or Western Arabic digits by matching them to the characters that immediately precede them. (And if no character precedes them, then it uses the ambient language.)

https://devblogs.microsoft.com/oldnewthing/?p=1653

The differences between the three options available under Use Native Digits are:

  • Context: The shape depends on the previous text in the same output.
  • Never: Gives full Unicode compatibility.
  • National: Native shapes determined by LOCALE_SNATIVEDIGITS.

Even though contextual digits substitution is commonly used for rendering in RTL environment, not all situations apply correctly when running into standardized terminology.

For example, HEX value should behave like other applications such as Adobe Illustrator or Paint 3D. Rendering should remain LTR with Western Arabic digits.

Set up the Arabic or Hebrew environment

Hebrew—ArcGIS Desktop

Keep the following in mind when setting up the Hebrew environment in ArcGIS Desktop:

  • According to information from Israel distributor, 95 percent of GIS users use English operating system, Hebrew OS is not required.
  • Set user and system locales to Hebrew (Israel) from Regional format.
  • Hebrew LP is not available for Desktop.

Hebrew—ArcGIS Pro

Keep the following in mind when setting up the Hebrew environment in ArcGIS Pro:

  • English OS
  • Set user and system locale to Hebrew (Israel)
  • Install ArcGIS Pro Hebrew Language Pack only from \\metro\ArcGISPro2.7_OnlyLanguagePacks\Hebrew
    or install a localized setup from \\metro\ArcGISPro2.7_LocalizedSetups
  • Start ArcGIS Pro, go to the back stage select Options, change ArcGIS Pro display language from English to Hebrew, restart the application will launch Hebrew ArcGIS Pro.

Arabic—ArcGIS Desktop

Keep the following in mind when setting up the Arabic environment in ArcGIS Desktop:

  • You need an Arabic OS to prevent running any false alarm. You can either deploy a VM from https://ecsprd.esri.com/vcac/#csp.cs.ui.catalog.list
    or install MS MUI from \\esri.com\software\OS\Microsoft\Windows\10\Languages
  • Install language packs or localized setups
    \\metro\ArcGIS10.8.1_OnlyLanguagePacks\Arabic
    \\metro\ArcGIS10.8.1_LocalizedSetups\Arabic

After installing the MUI, restart the desktop. If you have language packs installed, ArcGIS Desktop detects the registry and launches an Arabic Desktop. If you have set the editor permission, you can switch the language in ArcGIS Administrator, select Arabic from the drop-down menu, and relaunch Desktop.

Arabic - ArcGIS Pro

Keep the following in mind when setting up the Arabic environment in ArcGIS Pro:

  • Deploy an Arabic VM or Apply Arabic MUI.
  • Install language packs from \\metro\ArcGISPro2.7_OnlyLanguagePacks\Arabic
  • or install a localized set up from \\metro\ArcGISPro2.7_LocalizedSetups\Arabic.
  • Verify the user and system locale set to Arabic (SA).
  • The application launches Arabic ArcGIS Pro once it detects the language pack exists. If not, go to the backend and change the display language to Arabic.

A detailed workflow for set up can be found at VM and i18n machine set up.

Change the display language in Outlook

  1. Click File > Options > Language > Choose Display Language.
  2. Install the Arabic/Hebrew display language from Microsoft.
  3. Set Arabic as the default language and restart Outlook.

Set up a mobile device to test in Hebrew and Arabic

Set the iOS device language

Set the iOS device language using the following steps:

  1. Open Settings.
  2. Tap General.
  3. Tap Language and Region.
  4. Tap [device] language
  5. Select Hebrew or Arabic language from the list. Tap Done.
  6. Confirm your selection

After your device updates the language, it should return to the Home screen and display the language you selected.

Setting iOS device Region

Set the iOS device region using the following steps:

  1. Scroll to the bottom of the language list and tap Region.
  2. Search for Israel for Hebrew or Saudi Arabia for Arabic and tap Done.
  3. Confirm your selection.

After your device updates the region, it returns to the list of languages.

The regional format example can be seen at the bottom of the Language and Region page.

Set the Android device language

  1. Open Settings.
  2. Tap General management.
  3. Tap Language and Input.
  4. Tap Language.
  5. You can see the display languages that are installed on your Android device. If you don’t see Hebrew or Arabic in the list, tap Add a new language.
  6. Choose the language from the list and tap it.
    The language is now added to the list of the installed display languages.
  7. To change the active display language, drag the one you need to the top of the list.
  8. Tap Apply.

Set up the environment to test web apps in Hebrew and Arabic

ArcGIS Online does not inherit language settings from the operation system. Different apps inherit language settings differently.

ArcGIS Online and Portal UI

Regardless of privileges, users can sign in to their AGOL or Portal organization, click the user button in the upper right corner, and click My Settings. On the General tab, under Language, number, and date format, click Update and set the language.

It is recommend that you keep the My Settings tab opened and continue UI testing on a different tab. This allows you to come back to your settings quickly and change the language if needed.

Anonymous access to portals

Language is inherited from the browser settings or in some cases, from portal organization settings. It is a good practice to set organization settings matching the language of your target audience.

  1. Sign in to your portal as a member with admin privileges.
  2. Click Organization > Settings > Language and set the language to what is needed:

Access to configurable applications

Language is inherited from browser settings.

In Safari there is no way to set the language independent from the OS. On Apple machine, go to Settings > Language and set the language.

In Edge (Chromium), go to Settings > Language and set the language in the list. If you don’t see your language in the list, you can always add it. Don’t restart the browser as it will change all browser controls; just update the Settings tab and you will see all configurable apps opened in other tabs with the language you selected.

In Google Chrome, complete the following steps:

  1. In the upper right corner, click the menu button and select Settings.
  2. Click Advanced.
  3. Click Languages.
  4. Select a language and click the menu button.
  5. Check Display Google Chrome in this language.
  6. Click Relaunch.

In FireFox, click Options > General > Language. Don’t change the language set in the Choose the languages used to display menus, messages and notifications from Firefox drop-down menu. Click the Choose button for the Choose your preferred language for displaying pages options and drag and drop Arabic or Hebrew to the top of the list. If you don’t see the desired language in the list, you can click Add to add it there.

In other browsers, language settings are changed similarly.
